ABSTRACT
Renovation of existing buildings to meet Nearly Zero Energy Building (NZEB) standards is essential for reducing 
energy consumption and CO
2 
emissions in the residential sector, making it a central strategy for achieving na -
tional climate goals. While commonly applied renovation measures effectively reduce operational energy con -
sumption and CO
2 
emissions, they may increase the building ’ s environmental impact due to embodied emissions 
from construction materials and equipment. To address these opposing effects, this study employs a life cycle- 
oriented multi-objective optimization (MOO) approach to identify optimal renovation strategies for a repre -
sentative collective residential building. A Definitive Screening Design (DSD) is used to create a minimal 
simulation experiment, and regression analysis is applied to develop surrogate models that define the relation -
ships between primary energy consumption ( PE ), life cycle emissions ( LCE ), life cycle cost ( LCC ), and the 
considered renovation measures. These surrogate models are integrated into the MOO model, which is optimized 
using the NSGA-III algorithm to simultaneously minimize PE , LCE , and LCC . The resulting Pareto-optimal so -
lutions reveal distinct trade-offs, characterized by a non-linear positive relationship between PE and LCE , a linear 
negative relationship between PE and LCC , and a non-linear negative relationship between LCE and LCC . These 
relationships indicate that improvements in energy and environmental performance are associated with signif -
icant cost increases. To evaluate and rank the Pareto-optimal solutions, the hybrid Entropy-TOPSIS method is 
applied, resulting in the selection of an optimal renovation strategy with its corresponding set of renovation 
measures. The proposed methodology provides an efficient decision-support approach for renovating collective 
residential buildings following NZEB standards.
1. Introduction
The transition towards Nearly-Zero Energy Buildings (NZEB) is a key 
strategy for addressing global climate change and accelerating national 
energy transitions [1] . An NZEB is a building with very high energy 
performance, where a significant portion of its energy demand is sup -
plied by renewable sources [2] . To qualify a building as NZEB, national 
regulations are needed, outlining specific requirements for new and 
existing buildings, reflecting specific national, regional, or local condi -
tions. Common metrics for NZEB, include total, non-renewable or 
renewable primary energy ( PE ) expressed in kWh/(m
2
a) defined for 
different building categories [3,4] . In most cases, PE accounts for the 
energy consumption associated with heating, domestic hot water 
(DHW), ventilation, and cooling. However, extending the scope of this 
indicator to include the energy consumption of lighting and household 
appliances ensures a more comprehensive assessment of total energy 
demand, carbon footprint, and building environmental impact, leading 
to more optimal and sustainable renovation strategies [3] . Following, 
additional metrics are U values of building envelope, and CO
2 
emissions 
[3] . The European Union (EU) member states mandate that all new 
public buildings will comply with more stringent ZEB standards by 
2028, and all other building categories by 2030 [5] , while existing 
buildings are subject to progressive renovation requirements aligned 
with long-term climate neutrality targets. This legislative pathway is 
designed to accelerate the transition toward a highly energy-efficient 
and decarbonized building stock, aiming to reduce PE consumption to 
16% by 2030 and to 20 – 22% by 2035, with at least 55% of this reduction

incentive of the EU energy efficiency framework, resulting in optimized 
building energy performance and providing a more environmentally 
sustainable approach than a new construction [6] .
Most frequently used renovation scenario for residential buildings 
includes implementation of passive measures, aimed to improve the heat 
transfer coefficients of the building envelope and reduce infiltration, 
consequently minimizing overall energy consumption [7] . These mea -
sures include the use of high-performance thermal insulation, multi- 
glazed windows filled with inert gases such as argon, as well as the 
implementation of green roofs and external shading systems [8,9,10] . 
Passive renovation measures are considered as very effective strategies 
for reducing building energy consumption [11,12] , with thermal insu -
lation reducing heating demand by 40 – 60% [12] , and window 
replacement offering energy savings ranging from 23% in Mediterra -
nean climates to up to 77% in heating-dominated regions [13,14] . 
Active renovation measures are focused on the modernization of tech -
nical systems, including heating, DHW, cooling, and lighting. These 
measures typically integrate high-efficiency technologies and equip -
ment into the building technical system, including heat pumps, 
condensing boilers, Light-Emitting Diode (LED) lighting, and advanced 
building automation systems, to enhance energy performance and 
minimize energy consumption, with a potential energy savings of up to 
80% [11] . Integration of renewable energy into the building energy 
supply system significantly reduces PE consumption and CO
2 
emissions. 
The most implemented technologies include photovoltaic (PV) systems 
for on-site electricity generation, solar thermal panels for DHW, and, in 
recent applications, wind turbines [8] . Generally, installing PV systems 
on building roofs can lead to savings of total energy consumption 
ranging from 55% in apartments to 80% in single-family houses [15] . 
Moreover, when PV systems are combined with other building renova -
tion measures, total energy consumption in residential buildings can be 
reduced by more than 90% [16,17] .
Implementation of renovation measures and scenarios reduces 
operational energy consumption and CO
2 
emissions but can 
consequently increase the building ’ s overall environmental impact due 
to the embodied emissions associated with the materials and equipment 
used in the renovation process, building maintenance, as well as end-of- 
life demolition and disposal activities. This effect is particularly signif -
icant in cases of high renovation rates, which can lead to increased 
upfront GHG emissions if embodied emissions from materials and pro -
cesses are not adequately accounted [18] . To assess the environmental 
impacts of materials, equipment, and related processes throughout a 
building ’ s life-cycle, Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) is widely employed as 
a standardized analytical tool. The general framework for conducting 
LCA, as defined by ISO 14040:2006 and ISO 14044:2006, includes the 
following key phases: definition of objective and scope, life cycle in -
ventory analysis, impact assessment, and interpretation. System 
boundaries at the building level, are categorizing in four main stages of 
LCA: production (modules A1 – A3), construction (modules A4 – A5), use 
(modules B1 – B7) and end-of-life stage (modules C1 – C4), encompassing 
all life cycle emissions ( LCE ). The contribution of individual life-cycle 
modules to a building ’ s total environmental impact can vary signifi -
cantly, depending on the applied renovation measures, energy-saving 
potential, selected materials, climatic conditions, and the service life 
of both the building and its components. In colder climates, and building 
with high energy-saving potential, the operational energy use module 
(B6) can account for up to 90% of the building ’ s total life-cycle envi -
ronmental impact [19,20] . According to [21] , 75% of impacts originate 
from the operational energy use module (B6), 10% from material pro -
duction (A1 – A3), another 10% from components replacement (B4), and 
the remaining 5% from other modules (A4-A5, C1 – C4). Although repair 
(B2) and replacement (B4) contribute minimally to life-cycle emissions, 
they often represent a considerable portion of overall life-cycle costs 
( LCC ) [20] . Most studies, related to building renovations focus mainly 
on modules A1-A3, A4-A5, and B6, while other modules related to 
maintenance, replacement, repair, deconstruction, disposal, and reuse 
are often omitted [7] .

support informed decision-making and ensure the practical feasibility 
and scalability of renovation measures [12] . In some cases, large-scale or 
deep renovation strategies often require substantial upfront in -
vestments, where such investments may not always be economically 
feasible, as the resulting reductions in energy or fuel consumption do not 
sufficiently decrease operational energy costs to offset associated in -
vestments [22] . Therefore, in addition to minimizing primary energy 
consumption and environmental impacts through LCE , it is essential to 
ensure the economic effectiveness of the renovation process by mini -
mizing LCC , thereby supporting its long-term sustainability from both 
environmental and economic perspectives.
To simultaneously address multiple performance criteria and iden -
tify balanced and efficient building retrofit solutions, multi-objective 
optimization (MOO) algorithms are frequently employed. Kamazani 
et al. (2025) [23] optimized building orientation, window-to-wall ratio, 
and material selection for walls, roofs, and floors in an office building. 
Similarly, Benaddi et al. (2024) [24] applied MOO to classroom build -
ings to minimize LCC , LCE and thermal discomfort time, optimizing 
glazing type, window-to-wall ratio and shading. According to Alexakis 
et al (2025), operational energy and renovation cost remain the most 
frequently optimized objectives [25] . Research that involves life cycle 
emissions typically limits the analysis to operational emissions (B6), as 
seen in recent studies [26,27,28] . Other studies extend the system 
boundary to include material production (A1 – A3) and replacements 
(B4), such as the study by Arbulu et al. (2023) [29] . However, there still 
exists lack of comprehensive LCA of renovation measures for existing 
buildings. Kamazani et al. (2025) optimized an NZEB office building 
using a two-phase procedure, beginning with an energy-centric step and 
followed by a carbon-centric step [23] . This two-step design, however, 
restricts a fully integrated assessment of the interactions between energy 
and carbon objectives.
Among the various MOO algorithms proposed in the literature, 
evolutionary algorithms have been particularly effective in optimization 
of complex, non-linear building energy or renovation problems. While 
numerous MOO techniques exist, evolutionary algorithms are especially 
well suited for high-dimensional, non-linear problems with conflicting 
objectives. Their effectiveness in solving optimization problems with 
conflicting objectives without requiring gradient information makes 
them suitable for such applications. Within this class of methods, the 
Non-Dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm II (NSGA-II) is one of the 
most widely used MOO methods [25] . While NSGA-II produces solutions 
comparable to those of other optimization algorithms, the more robust 
NSGA-III has demonstrated improved optimization performance [25] . 
Alexakis et al. (2025) recommend a more frequent use of the NSGA-III 
algorithm to exploit its favorable characteristics for complex multi- 
objective problems [25] . Razmi at al. (2022) compared NSGA-II and 
NSGA-III in the multi-objective optimization of energy efficiency, ther -
mal comfort, and useful daylight illuminance in dormitory building 
design [30] . The results indicated that, despite slower initial conver -
gence, NSGA-III showed better overall performance than NSGA-II by the 
end of the optimization process. A recent study by Shi and Chen (2024) 
demonstrated the efficiency of NSGA-III in an energy-saving renovation 
framework for hospital buildings, optimizing energy consumption, 
renovation costs, and thermal comfort simultaneously [31] . By 
comparing NSGA-II, NSGA-III, and a decomposition-based multi-objec -
tive evolutionary algorithm, Chen et al. (2022) demonstrated that 
NSGA-III provides the most efficient optimization performance for 
building applications [32] .
Given the complexity of evaluating objective functions, MOO models 
often utilize surrogate models (SM), which enable significantly faster 
and more efficient assessment of building performance while incorpo -
rating the building ’ s physical model [33] . SM are simplified statistical or 
mathematical approximations of complex building simulation models, 
trained on a limited number of building performance simulations (BPS), 
which describe the functional relationship between design variables or 
building characteristics (inputs) and key performance indicators (out -
puts) [34] . Various types of SM have been proposed in the literature, 
ranging from polynomial [12,35] and multiple linear regression (MLR) 
[36] to artificial neural networks [31] . To generate the training dataset, 
several experimental design approaches are commonly employed, 
including design of experiments (DOE) techniques and Monte Carlo- 
based methods [34] . The number of BPS required to train a surrogate 
model can be significantly reduced by using an appropriate experi -
mental design approach [31] . For instance, a full factorial design (FFD) 
was successfully used to model final energy for heating and CO
2 
emis -
sions [12] , while the response surface methodology was effective at 
modeling building energy consumption [35] . While the proposed DOE 
techniques have proven to be both reliable and effective, there remains 
considerable potential for employing more efficient experimental de -
signs, particularly in scenarios involving a large number of design var -
iables. In this context, the Definitive Screening Design (DSD) represents 
a promising alternative, having demonstrated efficiency and robustness 
in achieving high predictive accuracy with a significantly reduced 
number of experiments [37,38] . Therefore, DSD presents a suitable 
option for the development of SM aimed at optimizing building reno -
vation strategies.
MOO algorithms generate a Pareto front of non-dominated solutions 
that are not always practical for decision-making. Therefore, an addi -
tional selection process is required to identify the most suitable solution, 
typically involving Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA) methods to 
rank and select the best solution from the Pareto front [26 19 39] .
1.1. Research gaps and study contributions
The renovation of existing buildings to comply with NZEB standards 
has become an increasingly important research topic, as evidenced by 
the substantial growth in published studies in recent years [11,40] . 
Nevertheless, several important gaps remain in the literature, including 
the following: 
⋅ First, most renovation studies assess either energy performance, LCE 
[41] , or LCC , while only a limited number of studies simultaneously 
integrate all three aspects in determining compliance with NZEB 
standards [25] .
⋅ Second, LCE assessments mainly focus on the material production 
stage (A1-A3) and operational emissions (B6), while phases such as 
maintenance, repair, replacement, and end-of-life treatment are 
often omitted [7] . This limited scope may lead to an underestimation 
of the total environmental impact and result in renovation strategies 
that seem effective in the short term but fail to provide sustainable 
performance throughout the building ’ s life cycle.
⋅ Third, previous studies show that LCE and LCC are rarely applied 
simultaneously within the same renovation study. While approxi -
mately 38% of renovation studies include LCE , only a much smaller 
share (around 16%) includes LCC analysis [25] , indicating that 
economic sustainability is not consistently addressed in long-term 
renovation decision-making.
⋅ Fourth, NZEB evaluations for residential sector are typically based on 
operational PE , commonly considering only heating and/or DHW, 
while only a limited number of studies consider appliances and 
lighting, even though excluding these end-uses can significantly 
underestimate total energy consumption [28] . Fifth, analyzed 
building is located in a continental climate zone, classified as Dfb 
according to the K ¨oppen climate classification, characterized by cold 
winters and warm summers [42] . Despite being one of the most 
prevalent climate zones in Central Europe, Dfb is among the least 
studied in the context of building renovation optimization research 
[34] .
⋅ Sixth, DSD is used to design the simulation experiment, enabling 
efficient exploration of the design space with a significantly reduced 
number of simulations. Seven input variables (5 continuous and 2

To address these gaps, this study introduces several contributions to 
the optimization of renovation strategies for existing residential build -
ings to meet NZEB standards. The analysis considers seven renovation 
measures, including passive and active measures, as well as the inte -
gration of renewable energy sources: 
⋅ A comprehensive LCE system boundary is determined, covering the 
majority of life cycle stages, including those usually excluded in 
literature (construction (A4-A5), use (B1-B5) and end-of-life (C1-C4) 
stages).
⋅ LCC is systematically assessed over the entire building lifetime, 
directly addressing the underrepresentation of long-term economic 
sustainability in existing renovation studies.
⋅ The modeled non-renewable PE includes heating, cooling, DHW, 
lighting, and appliances energy consumption, reduced by the elec -
tricity produced from an on-site renewable energy system. This in -
dicator represents building energy performance and compliance with 
NZEB standards following renovation.
⋅ By focusing on a building located in a Dfb continental climate, this 
study provides optimization results that address the limited repre -
sentation of this prevalent Central European climate zone in existing 
building renovation optimization studies, with findings transferable 
to similar buildings in comparable climatic conditions.
⋅ The DSD is applied for the first time in building renovation optimi -
zation context to design experiments and develop SM. Compared 
with conventional experimental designs, including FFD and RSM 
designs, this approach significantly reduces computational 
complexity and associated costs, with SM achieving high predictive 
accuracy.
⋅ A simultaneous optimization of PE , LCE , and LCC is performed using 
the state-of-the-art NSGA-III evolutionary algorithm, resulting in a 
set of Pareto-optimal solutions [30] .
⋅ Pareto-optimal solutions are evaluated and ranked using transparent 
and objective hybrid Entropy- TOPSIS method, with the optimal 
renovation scenario selected as the highest-ranked solution with its 
corresponding set of renovation measures.

2. Methodology
2.1. General overview
A brief overview of the methodology employed in this study is pre -
sented in Fig. 1 . This study employs MOO to minimize operational non- 
renewable PE consumption, LCE , and LCC in the renovation of repre -
sentative residential building to NZEB standards.
The building performance analysis is conducted on a representative 
building from the building block category, as defined in the National 
Building Typology Matrix (TABULA). The impact of seven renovation 
measures is evaluated, including installation of external wall and roof 
insulation, window replacement, lighting system replacement, installa -
tion of PV panels on the flat roof, shading installation, and replacement 
of the heating and DHW systems. These measures form the basis of a 
parametric study designed to assess their individual and combined ef -
fects on the overall building performance. DSD methodology is used to 
design a simulation experiment and a simulation matrix, in which each 
row represents a unique renovation scenario consisting of a specific 
combination of renovation measures. The DSD methodology is selected 
as it provides efficient exploration of the design space with a minimal 
number of required simulations, while still allowing for the estimation of 
the main, quadratic, and selected interaction terms. BPS are performed 
using DesignBuilder software, in which the representative building was 
modeled. The modelled energy consumption was subsequently validated 
using real operational energy data. Building performances, including PE 
and operational CO
2 
emissions, are estimated using the integrated 
EnergyPlus simulation engine, for renovation scenarios given in the 
simulation matrix. The calculation of LCE of selected materials and 
equipment is performed using Umberto software in combination with 
the Ecoinvent 3.9.1 database. For each renovation scenario, the LCC was 
calculated for the investment costs of renovation measures, as well as 
operational and maintenance costs over the assumed building lifetime.
Integration of the simulation matrix with corresponding simulated 
objective functions forms the dataset used for the development of single- 
objective functions ’ models (SM). For each objective function, which 
defines the relationship between design variables and building perfor -
mance indicators, a regression model is developed using multiple-linear 
regression (MLR) analysis. These models enable fast evaluation of 
various renovation scenarios without the need for repeated building 
performance simulations.
Determining the optimal renovation scenario requires the simulta -
neous optimization of all three single-objective functions, i.e., PE , LCE , 
and LCC . This is achieved by MOO using the NSGA-III algorithm, which 
generates a Pareto-optimal set of non-dominated renovation scenarios. 
However, for practical decision-making, selecting a subset of solutions is 
essential. One such approach involves MCDM methods to rank Pareto- 
optimal solutions based on predefined criteria. Among these, the TOP -
SIS method is applied to evaluate and rank solutions according to their 
proximity to an ideal scenario, considering the relative importance of 
each objective function. The final ranked list of renovation scenarios can 
help decision-makers in selecting the most suitable renovation scenario 
based on predefined objectives and priorities.

nalized and standardized collective residential buildings, primarily 
aimed at providing mass housing solutions through cost-effective, 
industrialized construction techniques. Architecturally, the building 
follows a simple, elongated rectangular layout with a flat roof, reflecting 
the principles of socialist-era housing design ( Fig. 2 .a). The building

2.4. Definitive screening design
BPS, based on fundamental physical laws, can accurately quantify 
the impact of numerous design variables (renovation measures) on 
operational energy use, as well as on indoor environmental conditions 
and HVAC system performance. Even though BPS provides detailed re -
sults, the computational cost becomes significant when a large number 
of simulations is required for optimization. In addition, BPS outputs are 
usually limited to operational indicators, and in multi-objective opti -
mization, other objective functions, such as PE , LCE , and LCC , cannot be 
obtained directly from BPS and must be derived through additional 
models or simulations. For example, PE , LCE , and LCC are calculated 
using models that combine BPS-based operational energy use with fuel- 
and electricity-specific conversion factors to derive PE , with embodied 
emissions data to determine LCE , and with capital, maintenance, and 
replacement cost data to determine LCC over the building ’ s life cycle. In 
the context of multi-objective optimization, running many simulations 
in BPS tools such as EnergyPlus, is highly demanding and computa -
tionally expensive. As an alternative, the proposed approach is to 
develop simplified regression models (SM) that relate building perfor -
mance to the relevant design variables. Using DOE methodology these

A class of DOE, known as DSD, was developed to provide small and 
highly efficient experimental designs that allow robust screening of 
main effects and estimation of quadratic effects, enabling reliable sur -
rogate model development and optimization [47,48,49] . DSD uses low, 
medium, and high factor levels in a structured way to describe the 
general shape of the response surface without exploring the full design 
space. A key advantage of DSD is that it can estimate all main effects 
without confounding and quadratic effects without aliasing with main 
effects. However, quadratic effects may be partially aliased with two- 
factor interactions. Two-factor interactions are not fully resolved, but 
potentially important interactions can still be identified. The main 
limitation of DSD is aliasing, since higher-order interactions cannot be 
distinguished and even two-way interactions remain partially corre -
lated. DSD therefore relies on the sparsity of effects principle, assuming 
that only a few factors have a dominant influence, and when many 
factors interact strongly, a DSD cannot represent the system accurately. 
Under conditions where sparsity of effects is present, DSD performs as a 
highly efficient response surface design [43] .

with long-term and intergenerational consequences [62,63] . Since our

fundamentally different cost determinants of IC , MC , and OC within the 
LCC assessment. IC and MC are predominantly impacted by the prices of 
construction materials, technical components, and equipment, which 
are mainly affected by inflation in core goods. OC are related to occu -
pants ’ routine use of the building and are largely associated with energy 
consumption for space heating and cooling, DHW, appliance use, and 
lighting, and are therefore primarily affected by energy price inflation. 
Recent studies emphasize that energy price inflation is distinct from core 
inflation, characterized by higher volatility, wider value ranges, and 
lower persistence. These characteristics suggest that treating energy 
related cost escalation separately from other cost components may be 
beneficial [64,65] .
This differentiated approach is consistent with similar methodolo -
gies in the building renovation studies that apply separate inflation [62]
or price escalation rates for energy and non – energy cost components 
[7,66,67] . Treating energy price inflation rate separately is particularly 
relevant in the context of Bosnia and Herzegovina, where current energy 
prices are substantially lower than EU averages (0.109 EUR/kWh for 
B & H and 0.289 EUR/kWh for EU) and are expected to rise.
To ensure alignment with local economic conditions, inflation rates 
are set in accordance with national forecasts of Central Bank of Bosnia 
and Herzegovina. (2025) [68] . Accordingly, an inflation rate of 3.5% 
was assigned to IC and MC , while a higher rate of 4% was assigned to OC 
accounting for anticipated energy price escalation.

To solve the MOO problem defined by (10), the NSGA-III algorithm is 
employed. NSGA-III is an evolutionary algorithm developed for MOO, 
extending the capabilities of its predecessor, NSGA-II [69] .While NSGA- 
II relies on crowding distance to preserve solution diversity, NSGA-III 
introduces reference point-based selection and objective space normal -
ization, which are more effective in high-dimensional objective spaces. 
By systematically distributing solutions using predefined reference 
points, NSGA-III achieves a more uniform approximation of the Pareto 
front and improved scalability in many-objective optimization prob -
lems. After executing the NSGA-III algorithm, a set of non-dominated 
solutions is obtained, forming the Pareto front. Each solution in this 
set represents a distinct compromise between the objectives, with no 
solution being superior to the others across all objectives.
2.7. Pareto front solutions ranking
As a result of MOO, the Pareto front with non-dominated solutions is 
obtained. For practical decision-making and implementation of a reno -
vation scenario, it is necessary to reduce the relatively large set of so -
lutions to a smaller subset. The convenient approach to Pareto front 
solution ranking is the employment of MCDM methods, which enable 
evaluation and ranking of alternatives based on multiple, often con -
flicting criteria. The employment of MCDM includes defining the sets of 
alternatives and evaluation criteria, and criteria relative importance 
(weights). The relative importance of criteria can be determined either 
subjectively or objectively. The subjective approach usually involves 
expert judgment, while the objective approach uses MCDM methods 
designed for objective weights estimation.
In this study, Pareto front solutions represent alternatives, while the 
single-objective functions represent the evaluation criteria. All criteria 
need to be minimized; thus, in terms of MCDM they represent costs. For 
the evaluation and ranking of alternatives, the TOPSIS method is 
employed. This method ranks alternatives based on their Euclidean 
distance to the positive and negative ideal solutions, considering the 
relative importance of each criterion [31,70] . The positive ideal solution 
represents a hypothetical alternative that achieves the best values for all 
criteria, while the negative ideal solution represents one that achieves 
the worst values. In this study, the positive ideal solution corresponds to 
the alternative with minimal PE , LCE , and LCC . According to the TOPSIS 
method, optimal solution should have the shortest distance from the 
positive ideal solution and the longest distance from the negative ideal 
solution. For estimation of the criteria's relative importance, the Entropy 
method is adopted [71] . The Entropy method assigns weights to criteria 
based on the variation of each criterion across the set of alternatives. The 
criteria with higher variability contain more information and are 
therefore assigned greater weights, while the criteria with lower vari -
ability contribute less to the decision-making process and thus smaller 
weights are assigned. Descriptions of Entropy and TOPSIS methods are 
provided in Appendix A.1 and A.2, respectively.

4. Discussion and limitations
4.1. Discussion
The study results show that implementing energy renovation mea -
sures can significantly reduce building operational PE , lowering it from a 
maximum pre-renovation value of 229 kWh/(m
2
a) to a minimum of 
58.55 kWh/(m
2

of performance aligns with NZEB standards in countries with similar 
continental climates, such as Austria, the Czech Republic, and Poland, 
where thresholds range between 68 and 75 kWh/(m
2
a) [72] . Even in 
scenarios where electricity is the sole energy source, and despite the 
high national primary energy conversion factor of 2.5, the results 
confirm that NZEB standards can still be achieved through the imple -
mentation of comprehensive renovation strategies. Calculated LCE over 
a 60-year building lifetime range from a maximum of 3.49 tCO
2
/m
2 
to 
1.35 tCO
2
/m
2 
for the highest-ranked renovation scenario. LCE includes 
both operational CO

renovation materials, equipment, construction, and deconstruction ac -
tivities. Calculated LCC , including investment, operational, and main -
tenance costs over a 60-year building lifetime, range from 1832 to 703 
EUR/m
2

certainty than LCE , primarily due to the higher variability of costs over 
the building lifetime [73] . This uncertainty is largely driven by fluctu -
ations in energy prices, which affect both investment and operational 
costs, and is further amplified by uncertainty related to the selection of 
discount rate [62] . As energy prices increase, renovation measures that 
minimize both investment and operational costs are more likely to be 
selected as optimal solutions [21] .
In the renovation scenario in which all measures are implemented at 
their highest level, operational emissions (module B6) account for the 
largest share of total LCE , reaching 78%. This confirms that operational 
emissions remain the dominant contributor to LCE over a 60-year 
building lifetime. The share of operational emissions in total LCE is 
consistent with values reported in the literature, where they typically 
contribute between 75% and 90% [19,21] . When considering embodied 
emissions, module B4 (replacement) accounts for the largest share, 
representing 50% of total embodied emissions and approximately 11% 
of total LCE , which exceeds the contributions of the production and 
construction stages. This result is associated with the frequent replace -
ment of windows, external shading systems, and the heat pump, high -
lighting that the replacement module should not be omitted when 
assessing LCE over the building lifetime [74] . Additionally, although 
material an equipment end of use stage contribute a relatively small 
share of embodied emissions (approximately 6.6%), they should not be 
excluded from the analysis, as the environmental impacts related to 
construction and demolition waste present substantial potential for 
improvement through enhanced material management and waste- 
reduction strategies [75] . Analysis of the impact of individual mea -
sures on embodied emissions, shows that windows contribute up to 
31.9% of total embodied emissions, followed by the heating and DHW 
system (20.9%), external shading systems (20.4%), and PV panels 
(18.5%).
The analysis shows that wall insulation thickness, window type, 
lighting system type, PV active surface area, and heating and DHW 
system type influence all three objectives. Increasing wall insulation 
thickness generally reduces PE , LCE , and LCC . However, this measure 
also exhibits nonlinear effects that slightly increase PE and LCE , never -
theless, these contributions are significantly smaller compared to the 
reductions achieved through the dominant linear effect. LCC decreases 
progressively as wall insulation thickness increases, confirming the cost- 
effectiveness of this renovation measure. These findings are consistent 
with previous studies where it is concluded that optimal solutions 
consistently include maximum insulation thickness, particularly in 
colder climates and in scenarios prioritizing minimization of LCC [27] . 
Additionally, in study [76] , insulation thickness and insulation material 
are identified as the most influential parameters in building renovation, 
concluding that adding external wall insulation represents the most 
effective renovation option [21] . Window replacement reduces PE and 
LCE but increases LCC due to high costs and frequent replacements. 
Adding roof insulation decreases LCE without affecting PE or LCC . The 
lighting upgrades moderately reduce all objectives, while installation of 
PV panel substantially decreases all objectives. External shading has no 
impact on PE but increases LCE and LCC , indicating its ineffectiveness 
for this climate. Replacing the heating and DHW system with a heat 
pump substantially decreases all objectives, emphasizing its importance 
for achieving optimal building performance across all objective 
functions.

effectiveness is strongly influenced by local climate conditions [80,81] .

LCE shows that, in the optimal renovation scenario (highest-ranked 
solution), operational emissions (module B6) account for 83% of total 
LCE, while embodied emissions contribute the remaining 17%. Among 
the embodied emissions, module B4 (replacement) represents the largest 
share (49%), followed by the production and construction stages 
(A1 – A5), which account for 38%. Analysis of the impact of individual 
measures on embodied emissions shows that windows contribute with 
39.0% of total embodied emissions, followed by heating and DHW 
system (26.9%), PV panels (18.4%) and wall insulation with 10.6%.
The results show that an optimal renovation strategy for renovating 
multifamily residential buildings to NZEB standards can be identified 
through a life cycle-based optimization approach. These findings are 
particularly relevant for buildings in continental climate regions and 
provide valuable insights for planning and implementation of future 
sustainable renovation projects.
4.2. Limitations
In estimations of PE , LCE , and LCC , temporal effects such as the aging 
and performance deterioration of building components and equipment 
were not considered. However, it is reasonable to expect that building 
performance will decline over time as materials degrade, until compo -
nents are replaced or renovated. Following renovation, performance is 
generally restored to its initial level. Considering aging and degradation 
effects over the building ’ s lifetime could enhance the accuracy of energy 
use, carbon emissions (both embodied and operational), and cost as -
sessments, providing a more comprehensive evaluation of long-term 
performance.
Although all LCA modules were included in the analysis, one limi -
tation of this study is the use of a static electricity mix for the entire 
building lifetime. The electricity mix is expected to shift toward re -
newables, which would reduce the carbon intensity of operational 
emissions while increasing the relative importance of embodied im -
pacts. Future research should therefore examine how changes in the 
electricity mix influence the selection of renovation measures [7,22] . 
Additionally, while this study introduced a novel integration of emis -
sions and cost into a unified assessment method, the inclusion of carbon 
footprint compensation costs in total LCC calculations needs further 
investigation to assess its impact on overall renovation [82] .
The use of a single ranking method, such as TOPSIS, or a single 
weights estimation method, such as Entropy, may be sufficient for 
decision-making. However, the integration of multiple methods, 
particularly those employing different evaluation approaches, such as 
those evaluating alternatives based on the distance from the ideal, 
compromise, and average solution, as well as approaches with alterna -
tive outranking, can improve the robustness and accuracy of final 
rankings. Similarly, an ensemble approach for weights estimation, 
which combines multiple methods, can result in more robust and reli -
able criterion weights estimates.

5. Conclusion
This study presents the multi-objective optimization of residential 
building renovation to NZEB standards, considering primary energy 
consumption, life cycle emissions, and life cycle costs. A novel simula -
tion experiment design based on Definitive Screening Design (DSD) is 
introduced to ensure a time and computationally efficient optimization 
process. The hybrid Entropy-TOPSIS method is applied to prioritize 
objectives and rank renovation scenarios, enabling the selection of an 
optimal renovation strategy with its corresponding set of measures.
The key findings of the study can be summarized as follows: 
- DSD was applied for the first time in the context of building reno -
vation optimization to NZEB standards for an efficient simulation 
experiment design and development of regression-based surrogate 
models. This approach proved to be adequate and time and compu -
tationally efficient, requiring only 18 simulations to evaluate the 
effects of multiple renovation measures on the objective functions.
- The NSGA-III algorithm was applied to simultaneously optimize 
primary energy consumption, life cycle emissions, and life cycle 
costs, successfully converging to a uniformly distributed Pareto 
front. The Pareto front reveals clear trade-offs among the objectives.
- The optimization results confirmed that achieving high energy and 
environmental performance in renovated buildings requires sub -
stantial financial investment. However, the results also confirmed 
the long-term viability and sustainability of residential building 
renovation projects.
- Analysis of Pareto-optimal solutions showed that window replace -
ment and roof insulation installation provide the greatest flexibility 
in level selection without affecting solution optimality. External wall 
insulation and lighting system replacement provide moderate flexi -
bility, while PV module installation, shading system installation, and 
heating and DHW system replacement remain fixed, representing 
measures with limited flexibility and low influence on decision- 
making in defining the renovation strategy.
- The optimal renovation strategy proposes the installation of a min -
imum of 16.5 cm of insulation on external walls and 11 cm on the 
roof, replacement of the existing windows with triple-glazed win -
dows, upgrade of the lighting system, installation of PV modules on 
the maximum available flat roof area, and replacement of the district 
heating system with a heat pump. External shading installation is not 
feasible and therefore not recommended.
- The highest-ranked renovation scenarios demonstrate that imple -
mentation of energy renovation measures can reduce operational 
primary energy consumption below 60 kWh/(m
2
a), life cycle emis -
sions below 1.4 tCO
2
/m
2
, and life cycle costs below 850 EUR/m
2
.
The study demonstrates that it is possible to identify renovation 
scenarios that meet NZEB standards using a life cycle-based multi- 
objective optimization approach. The study findings are relevant for 
similar residential buildings in continental climate regions. Future 
research should investigate the effects of material and equipment 
degradation, changes in the electricity mix, and the integration of car -
bon compensation costs into life cycle cost calculations.
