A B S T R A C T
Building energy modeling (BEM) are key to optimizing building performance. While data-driven BEM models 
have significantly accelerated this process, they have limitations in balancing modeling accuracy and 
component-level interpretability. This study proposes a graph attention network (GAT) for editable component- 
based building energy prediction and feature interpretation analysis. We used 3,000 residential buildings with 
geometric and energy usage information via a parametric method and EnergyPlus. By treating building com­
ponents (walls, doors, windows, etc.) as nodes and their physical connections as edges. We created a knowledge 
embedding method to represent the 3D model and thermal relationships of real-world. An Enhanced GAT was 
used for graph-level regression, and GCNExplainer was applied for component-level interpretation.The results 
show a 13% accuracy improvement over end-to-end models under the climatic conditions of Changsha, China. 
The interpretability analysis reveals that geometric features and component connections have a greater impact 
on energy usage than material performance. Finally, a practical case demonstrates the proposed method’s po­
tential for energy-efficient design and retrofit. This work highlights the importance of spatial information 
embedding in BEM and validates its feasibility in decision-making.
1. Introduction
In 2022, the International Energy Agency (IEA) reported that 
building operations accounted for 30 % of global final energy con­
sumption and 27 % of total energy-related CO2 emissions in 2021. 
Among these, 8 % came from direct emissions, and 19 % resulted from 
electricity and heat production [1]. Residential buildings, compared to 
non-residential ones, showed higher energy consumption and CO2 
emissions [2]. Globally, the construction industry is transitioning from 
prioritizing new developments to focusing on the existing building 
stock. To achieve carbon neutrality, it is necessary not only to pursue 
low-energy design strategies for new buildings but also to promote green 
retrofitting of existing ones [3].
Many studies have focused on energy reduction through improve­
ments in building operations [4,5], building materials [6,7], and occu­
pant behavior [8,9]. However, relatively little attention has been paid to 
how spatial design affects energy use. Research has shown that spatial 
configuration can have a more significant impact on building energy 
performance than other design factors. Considerable energy savings can 
be achieved through improved spatial efficiency and well-planned 
architectural layouts [10], indicating the need for evaluation ap­
proaches that integrate spatial efficiency into green building design and 
retrofitting.
Building Energy Modeling (BEM) has been widely applied in both 
academic research and practical engineering for low-carbon and net- 
zero building design. In this study, data from BEM are used to 
construct a Building Component Graph System (BCGS). Comparing 
graph-based Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) with 2D array-based ma­
chine learning models, the proposed system shows superior energy 
consumption prediction and reliable real-world application.
2. Background
2.1. Building energy modeling (BEM)
In general, common BEM approaches can be categorized into three 
types: (1) physics-based (white-box), (2) data-driven (black-box), and 
(3) combined physics and data-driven (gray-box).
Mature physics-based models include DOE-2 [11], EnergyPlus [12], 
and TRNSYS [13]. These models leverage computational power to

simulate multiphysics systems governed by complex physical laws. 
Their primary advantage lies in the high fidelity of physical simulations. 
However, they require a large amount of detailed input data, including 
building physical parameters, environmental conditions, and opera­
tional settings, to solve dynamic balance equations. The results are 
sensitive to the accuracy of these inputs, and the computational burden 
is often significant. In the era of big data, this limitation has led to the 
rise of data-driven approaches that aim to bypass complex physics-based 
calculations while maintaining reliability.
Black-box models have benefited from the rapid advancement of 
machine learning (ML) and artificial intelligence (AI) over the past two 
decades. ML algorithms use internal hidden layers and activation func­
tions to capture nonlinear relationships in data. In recent years, there 
has been growing research interest in using ML for building energy 
prediction [15]. Common ML techniques encompass decision tree 
models [16], ensemble learning approaches [17], Bayesian networks 
[14], graph neural networks [18], deep neural networks [19,20], and 
recurrent neural networks [21]. Although black-box models can deliver 
rapid computations and achieve satisfactory accuracy, they also come 
with inherent limitations. Black-box models are fast and accurate but 
require structured 2D data and lack robustness in extrapolation with 
missing samples. The opacity of these models and the inflexible design of 
early standalone ML algorithms continue to limit their adaptability.
The gray-box model, combines physical priors (white-box) and data- 
driven calibration (black-box). A classic example is the RC network 
model, which compares building thermodynamics to an electrical cir­
cuit, using thermal resistance for heat transfer and thermal capacitance 
for heat storage. The model structure is based on physical knowledge 
(white-box), while key parameters are calibrated with historical data 
(black-box). This approach ensures interpretability and reduces reliance 
on detailed data and computational complexity.
Building on the gray-box model, Raissi et al. proposed the Physics- 
Informed Neural Network (PINN) to better integrate physical knowl­
edge with machine learning, incorporating nonlinear partial differential 
equations (PDEs) into supervised learning tasks [22]. This sparked the 
emergence of knowledge-embedded machine learning, commonly 
referred to as Physics-Informed Machine Learning (PIML). PIML enables 
the integration of physical knowledge into ML models at various stages, 
including inputs, architecture, and outputs. These methods go beyond 
correcting physical formulas to include physically inspired, guided, in­
tegrated, or aware network designs. Any approach that improves the 
physical consistency of ML predictions can be regarded as knowledge- 
embedded [23].
For example, Chen proposed a component-based machine learning 
(CBM) framework that encodes building structure knowledge as se­
mantic information within the model architecture. This approach 
significantly improved robustness in building energy prediction [24]. 
Similarly, Jiang developed a modularized neural network (ModNN) that 
incorporates physical priors such as heat balance equations and con­
sistency constraints into a data-driven architecture, enabling cross- 
building generalization [25].
However, existing knowledge embedding methods focus on the 
building scale, making it difficult to analyze component-level physical 
mechanisms, which limits model generalization and accuracy. There­
fore, a modeling approach that ensures physical consistency and enables 
component-level resolution is required.
2.2. Graph data and graph modeling
2.2.1. Implicit representation of architectural space
ML can learn from data without predefined rules. This gives them the 
potential to simulate human-like design thinking in generating spatial 
layouts. However, the learning process is often implicit and lacks logical 
or interpretable representations of spatial structures. Current research 
mainly focuses on two-dimensional (2D) spatial analysis. Typical tasks 
include floor plan generation [26–28], recognition and error detection 
[27], and façade rendering [29]. Some methods try to extract height 
information using depth maps or sliced 2D images [30,31], but these 
often result in incomplete or missing three-dimensional (3D) details.
Recent research has begun to explore 3D architectural representa­
tions using formats such as voxels [32], point clouds [33], neural radi­
ance fields [34], and function-based representations [31]. However, 
these methods often lack interpretability and operational control, which 
limits the application of ML in 3D spatial reasoning [35].
Architectural design involves many types of heterogeneous data. In 
energy optimization, design workflows require methods that are precise, 
detailed, flexible, and easy to control. To meet these needs, topological 
representations are essential. They help capture the full 3D spatial re­
lationships within buildings.
2.2.2. Graph for spatial knowledge embedding
Graph is a specialized data structure that offers new ways to manage 
complex relationships in architecture and urban design. It also expands 
how ML can be used in design and engineering. Unlike 2D pixels or 3D 
voxels, graphs capture both detailed elements (nodes) and their logical 
connections (edges), preserving the structural logic of spatial layouts.
Graph-based methods are effective in capturing topology, hierarchy, 
and relationships between elements, especially in floor plan analysis. 
For example, Nelson Nauata et al. proposed a graph-constrained 
generative adversarial network for generating residential floor plans 
[36]. Ramon Elias Weber et al. developed a hypergraph model to 
represent spatial organization in floor plans, enabling geometric opti­
mization for better building performance [10]. In 3D applications, 
Zhong et al. introduced the Building-GNN framework, which supports 
designer interaction and co-design for generating voxel-based building 
prototypes [32].
2.3. BEM using graph neural networks
In low-carbon BEM, GNNs provide a promising approach to better 
capture the underlying building physics. Building components and their 
interconnections significantly affect energy performance through con­
duction, convection, and radiation. Recent studies have demonstrated 
this potential. For example, Hu et al. constructed urban building graphs 
based on solar shading relationships and improved the accuracy of city- 
scale energy prediction [20]. Jia et al. represented building thermal 
zones as graph nodes and enhanced thermal load forecasting [18]. These 
pioneering studies highlight the potential of graph structures in 
modeling complex spatial dependencies. However, they generally rely 
on specific relational assumptions, such as shading or zone partitioning, 
and remain at macro scales where physical consistency with real designs 
is still limited.
Component-level influences are particularly significant. For 
example, interior partitions can modify daylight distribution and airflow 
patterns, thereby influencing lighting and ventilation performance and, 
ultimately, the overall building energy consumption.. Therefore, relying 
only on bulk thermal parameters to build end-to-end metamodels [37] 
cannot fully address the complexities of new construction, retrofitting, 
and extension projects. To overcome this limitation, we propose a 
method that encodes both the connectivity and thermo-physical attri­
butes of components. Nodes represent major interior and exterior ele­
ments including walls, roofs, slabs, doors, and windows. Unlike previous 
approaches that require all graphs to contain the same number of nodes, 
our formulation allows variable-sized graphs. The model predicts whole- 
building EUI at the graph level, while retaining flexibility and inter­
pretability at the component level.
However, performance evaluation alone cannot fully inform design 
decisions in real-world practice. Architects and engineers must often 
navigate trade-offs between energy performance, financial feasibility, 
and occupant comfort. These criteria are not only interrelated but also 
inherently conflicting—improvements in one often come at the cost of 
another. The integration of multi-objective optimization (MDO) is not

Energy use, construction cost, and daylight availability were chosen

2.4. Research objectives
In summary, this study identifies three critical research gaps that 
remain unaddressed: 
The lack of data representation methods capable of preserving both 
the three-dimensional detail and spatial attributes of buildings.
Limited model flexibility in adjusting structural details, which con­
strains applicability in real-world engineering.
A shortage of knowledge-informed models that can simultaneously 
ensure both prediction accuracy and interpretability.
We acknowledge that the thermal processes of a single building are 
physically explicit and interpretable. However, within high-dimensional 
design spaces that involve numerous variable parameters, the relation­
ship between design decisions and performance outcomes becomes 
highly complex and difficult to interpret directly. Traditional physics- 
based simulations provide accurate results but impose a high compu­
tational cost, which limits their feasibility for large-scale exploration. 
The primary goal of this study is to develop a data-driven framework 
that integrates predictive efficiency with transparent decision logic, 
enabling rapid evaluation and optimization within architectural design 
practice.
To address these challenges, this research proposes the Building 
Component Graph System(BCGS). The core advantage of it is the ability 
to replace components flexibly without affecting the overall structure. 
Although explicit mechanical constraints are not directly embedded, the 
structural encoding implicitly preserves physical consistency. The model 
considers the effects of internal partitions on solar radiation and airflow. 
It also represents three-dimensional features, including vertical differ­
ences between spaces. These improvements increase modeling accuracy. 
The model is tested in a typical residential unit. When combined with a 
multi-objective optimization algorithm, it shows clear potential for 
practical use in energy retrofit design. We developed an interactive web 
platform (https://bcgs.work/).
3. Method
This study aims to predict and optimize residential building energy 
use through BCGS, in which nodes represent architectural components 
and edges denote their physical connections. 3,000 residential models 
with detailed component parameters were sampled using Planfinder and 
simulated using the Ladybug within the Grasshopper platform. Each 
node encodes geometric, thermal, and surface-level attributes, enabling 
the graph to reflect both spatial partitions and construction heteroge­
neity. We then apply a GNN for energy prediction, comparing its per­
formance against alternative GNN architectures and conventional end- 
to-end models. To enhance interpretability, GNNExplainer identifies 
key components and influential features via structural and feature-level 
perturbations (Fig. 1). Finally, Wallacei is employed for multi-objective 
optimization on a representative residential unit, jointly balancing EUI, 
construction cost, and useful daylight illuminance (UDI), thereby 
demonstrating the model’s practical relevance in energy-conscious 
design.
3.1. Data Setup
3.1.1. Data collection
This study focuses on residential buildings, with an emphasis on 
single-unit apartments, which represent the largest share of total 
building energy consumption [2]. The analysis excludes overall building 
layout, structural systems, and mechanical systems such as HVAC. All 
data were generated within Rhino 8 using the Grasshopper platform and 
the Planfinder plugin [41]. Planfinder is an industrial-grade tool that 
utilizes a floor plan database to algorithmically retrieve and adapt 
existing layouts for new design configurations. By specifying contours 
and entry points, users can generate viable residential floor plans 
through a parametric process that has been validated in previous studies 
[42,43]. The purpose of using Planfinder was to rapidly generate diverse 
yet rational floor plan samples for model training and validation. This 
stage represents a sampling process, focusing on diversity and ratio­
nality, rather than a continuous parametric design transformation. 
Using this method, we generated 1,000 unique floor plans, each anno­
tated with labeled functional zones (Fig. A1).
We applied two experimental treatments to each of the 1,000 floor 
plans. The first removed all interior walls, keeping only the outer en­
velope. This was done to test how interior partitions affect energy pre­
dictions. The second kept an average ceiling height, but varied the 
heights of different functional zones. This helped assess the error caused 
by assuming uniform height.
These treatments produced three data groups: 
• None – no interior walls, no height variation;
• Interior Wall – with interior walls, but uniform height;
• Interior Wall & Height – with both interior walls and height 
variation.
In total, we generated 3,000 residential samples with diverse spatial 
characteristics (Fig. 2). To ensure consistency across the dataset, all 
functional zones were assigned identical operational settings (Table 1). 
These configurations are based on the DOE Commercial Prototype 
Building Models and ASHRAE Standard 90.1 for midrise apartments. 
They include standardized temperature setpoints, HVAC schedules, in­
ternal loads, occupancy patterns, and equipment usage profiles (see 
Supplementary Materials).
This study focuses on testing the feasibility of the proposed BCGS 
framework, so generalized climate adaptation is not yet considered. All 
simulations use the EPW weather file for Changsha, China (28.2282◦N, 
112.9388◦E), obtained from the Ladybug EPW map. Changsha has a 
humid subtropical monsoon climate, with hot and humid summers and 
cold, damp winters. These conditions place high thermal demands on 
building envelopes. Future studies will extend the framework to other 
climate zones.
All 3,000 samples were simulated individually using the Ladybug 
plugin in Grasshopper to generate accurate, physics-based energy data. 
The simulation relies on two established engines: EnergyPlus for energy 
modeling and Radiance for daylight analysis. Both are widely recog­
nized in the BEM research community.

In the BCGS, building components are encoded as graph nodes, while 
their physical connections are modeled as edges (Fig. 3). Each node 
contains three types of attributes: geometric, thermal, and sub-surface

introduced to represent wall geometry in a consistent form. As Plan­

Thermal properties include type, conductivity, thickness, and 
absorptance values for thermal, solar, and visible radiation. These at­

addition, the structural classification—such as exterior wall, interior 
wall, floor, or roof—implicitly encodes the spatial position of each 
component.
The Subsurface information shows how building components are 
encoded as graph nodes, including common types such as windows and 
doors. By defining subsurface type, area, thermal transmittance, and 
SHGC, the details of the surface can be flexibly replaced while main­
taining strong physical consistency.
In the edge configuration, all component-to-component connections 
are assigned a value of 1 to expand the range of influence while mini­
mizing computational complexity.
Traditional end-to-end models rely on homogenized parameters for 
energy features, such as uniform thermal conductivity and averaged 
window-to-wall ratios, which limits their ability to capture vertical 
spatial variations. In contrast, graph-based structures handle heteroge­
neous data more effectively. Internal partitions can be added or removed 
by modifying graph nodes, and component-specific thermal properties 
or window configurations can be flexibly replaced without altering the 
overall model structure.
All data were generated using the Ladybug plugin on the Grass­
hopper platform in Rhino 8. For each of the 3,000 design cases, 
component parameters were randomly sampled within predefined 
ranges (Table 2), and the resulting values were assigned to individual 
elements. Using Python scripts in Grasshopper, physical adjacency be­
tween components were then parsed and converted into graph edges.

model includes two graph attention layers and two fully connected 
prediction layers. It also contains two layer normalization modules, an 
average-max pooling layer, and a dropout module for regularization.

tions, multi-head attention, and skip connections (Fig. 4). The model 
was implemented in Python using the PyTorch and PyTorch Geometric 
libraries. Training was performed on a system with an Intel Core i7 
processor and an NVIDIA RTX 3050Ti GPU.

models use identical training configurations (see Supplementary

The predictive performance of the proposed GNNs is further 
compared with end-to-end data-driven models. Among them, XGBoost is 
widely used in BEM for energy prediction but only supports 2D array 
inputs [45]. To accommodate this limitation, two variants are 
constructed. 
• XGBoost Simplified includes averaged parameters such as Area and 
Conductivity for Ground & Roof, Exterior Wall, Interior Wall, and the 
Window-to-Wall Ratio (WWR).
• XGBoost Refined further incorporates the Shape Index (SI) and dis­
aggregates Exterior Wall and WWR by orientation (Table 4).
3.2.3. Gnnexplainer
To enhance model interpretability, this study adopts GNNExplainer 
as a model-agnostic tool for local analysis of graph-level regression re­
sults [46]. GNNExplainer is independent of the underlying model’s 
architecture or parameters. It identifies critical associations between 
input features and predictions by optimizing input perturbations and 
applying feature masking (Fig. 4). Compared to gradient-based methods, 
GNNExplainer avoids the problem of gradient saturation [47]. It also 
overcomes the limitations of feature-only methods, which cannot 
explain discrete structural changes in graphs.
In the node feature analysis, GNNExplainer applies both positive and 
negative perturbations to each feature and measures their average effect 
on the prediction. It also uses node masks to quantify the contribution of 
each node to the model’s output. The results from feature perturbation 
and node masking are combined to generate an overall importance 
score.

3.3. Graph centrality metrics
To better understand how the GNN utilizes building graph structures, 
we introduce four graph centrality metrics (Table 5). These are 
compared with the node importance scores obtained from GNNEx­
plainer to analyze their correlation. Centrality metrics quantify a node’s 
role based on connectivity, mediation, and information flow. This allows 
us to examine whether the GNN relies more on structural hubs for en­

3.4. Case study application
This section verifies the model’s potential to evolve from a predictive 
tool into a decision-support system. A multi-objective optimization is 
conducted on a typical residential unit, aimed at real engineering 
problems rather than data generation. The results demonstrate the 
flexibility of the BCGS structure in component-level adjustments and 
show how model interpretation can guide design strategies, forming a 
closed loop from model understanding to design empowerment.
The floor plan is shown in Fig. 5, with a uniform ceiling height of 3 
m. Exterior walls are fixed and cannot be altered, while interior wall­
s—except those enclosing the bathroom—are adjustable within a pre­
defined range. If an interior wall overlaps with an exterior wall, it is 
treated as absent. Window size is controlled by the window-to-wall 
ratio; a ratio of zero indicates that no window is present on that wall. 
Interior doors must be anchored to their corresponding interior walls, 
and their presence is encoded as a binary value (0 or 1) along with 
position information. All parametric modeling was implemented using 
the Grasshopper platform in Rhino 8.

given point remains within a visually comfortable range. It is one of the

4. Result
4.1. Validation of homogenization-related errors
Fig. 6 shows that the three groups of 1,000 samples display clear 
differences in energy use during the initial data collection phase. All 
parameters are kept consistent across groups, with the only variations 
being the presence of interior walls and height differences. Exterior wall 
properties and sub-surface attributes remain identical.
Results show that the group without interior walls or height differ­
ences (None) has the lowest overall EUI, while the group with both 
interior walls and height variation (Interior Wall & Height) consistently 
records the highest. The same trend is observed in both heating and 
cooling loads. This result comes from extra thermal paths and uneven 
temperatures caused by internal partitions and height differences. These 
factors make HVAC systems work harder. In contrast, uniform spaces 
allow easier and more efficient thermal control.
Using the Interior Wall & Height group as the baseline, the Interior 
Wall group underestimates energy use by an average of 3.1 %. Ignoring 
both height variation and interior partitions (None) leads to a 7.4 % 
underestimation. These results suggest that neglecting internal details 
can substantially underestimate building energy consumption, particu­
larly in traditional models that rely on geometric simplification or 
averaged parameters.
4.2. Model performance comparison
We trained four models—Base GCN, GIN, Base GAT, and Enhanced 
GAT—for 1,000 epochs using identical hyperparameters. Accuracy was 
evaluated with Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE), Mean Absolute Error 
(MAE), and R2. Training time was recorded as the cost indicator. The 
comparison used only the Interior Wall & Height group (1,000 graph 
samples). Results for the other groups and their combinations are pro­
vided in the supplementary materials.
As shown in Table 6, the XGBoost Refined model, which includes 
more input parameters, shows slightly higher accuracy than XGBoost 
Simplified. Although the Enhanced GAT model requires longer training 
due to its complexity, this one-time cost yields about a 13 % improve­
ment in prediction accuracy. This improvement may result from the fact 
that XGBoost lacks detailed component-level features and cannot 
represent connectivity through two-dimensional arrays, which leads to a 
significant loss of input information.
4.3. GCNExplainer’s interpretation
4.3.1. Global feature importance
By applying GCNExplainer to perturb and mask all node features, we 
measured the contribution of each component attribute to energy pre­
diction. The absolute values of these contributions are shown in Fig. 7. 
Component Area has the strongest effect, with an average contribution 
of 26 %, a maximum of 34 %, and a minimum above 10 %. Sub-surface 
Area also plays a role, typically ranging from 5 % to 9 %. Thermal 
properties, however, show little influence on the model output. In 
contrast, the SI, a geometric feature, contributes around 5 %. Overall, 
geometry features like area and SI have a strong impact on thermal 
exchange and energy use.
4.3.2. Feature importance by component type
All components were classified into 14 types. These categories are 
not mutually exclusive. For example, exterior walls can be grouped by 
orientation (east, south, west, north) or by sub-surface configuration (no 
openings, door only, window only, or both). Fig. 8 presents the feature 
importance statistics across these categories.
The results show that Area contributes up to 55 % for roofs and 
floors, followed by exterior walls, but its impact on interior walls is 
relatively small. This provides component-level detail not captured in 
the global analysis of Section 4.3.1. In contrast, the SI is more important 
for interior walls than for roofs, floors, or exterior walls.
Subsurface area reveals additional effects. Windows contribute much 
more than doors. Walls facing east and south are more sensitive to 
window area. This agrees with real-world thermal behavior, since 
windows have lower thermal resistance than doors. Their size variation 
has a stronger impact on indoor temperature, especially on east and 
south façades exposed to higher solar radiation.
Subsurface Conductivity contributes nearly 8 % in exterior walls 
with windows. This indicates that the model captures the thermal 
relevance of transparent envelope elements. However, other features, as 
noted in Section 4.3.1, show only limited influence (Fig. A2).
In summary, the global analysis in Section 4.3.1 masks important 
differences between component types. To understand model behavior 
accurately and to support effective design optimization, feature impor­
tance should be examined in relation to specific component categories 
and their physical roles.
4.3.3. Importance of component connectivity
Edges are critical because they enable information to propagate 
across the graph. In this study, edges are encoded as binary values, but 
GCNExplainer can still assess their impact by applying a masking 
approach. This reveals hidden thermal pathways and key design junc­
tions. The results are shown in Fig. 9. Note that not all components can 
be connected; for example, roofs and floors are never directly linked.
Connections involving exterior walls have the strongest impact on 
the model’s predictions. Among them, connections between exterior 
walls are especially important because they determine envelope conti­
nuity and influence thermal bridging. When sub-surfaces such as win­
dows or doors are present, these junctions often become thermal weak 
points. For instance, the edge between an east-facing wall and a 
windowed wall shows high importance, suggesting that window

were clustered into three groups using the K-means algorithm (Fig. 11). 
Representative spatial solutions were analyzed based on the centroid of 
each cluster. 
• Most solutions (Cluster1, Cluster2, Cluster3) optimized performance 
by enhancing south-facing window glass and increasing insulation 
thickness on the west-facing roof.
• Cluster1 reduced the number of interior walls to improve spatial 
openness, achieving a trade-off between UDI and construction cost.

5. Discussion
5.1. Findings on BCGS
The BCGS data structure improves component-level modeling 
accuracy by 13 % compared with end-to-end models. This 
improvement comes from its stronger geometric representation and 
greater physical consistency. Compared with Base GCN, GAT introduces 
self-attention mechanisms that capture complex radiative interactions 
among components. Experiments show that GAT raises test set perfor­
mance from R2 = 0.613 to R2 = 0.842, a 40 % gain. The Enhanced GAT

increasing graph complexity also raises computational cost, requiring a 
balance between accuracy and efficiency.
Geometric features, especially component area, dominate the 
impact on energy consumption. This suggests that traditional retro­
fitting strategies focused on material and thickness may need to be 
reevaluated. GCNExplainer analysis shows that Area is the most influ­
ential factor across all component types, contributing more than 50 % in 
roofs and floors. This supports the common use of per-unit-area energy 
metrics in energy policy research [48,49], but also highlights the over­
looked role of façade area. Such findings may guide the development of 
new composite indicators, such as an “effective room volume” that in­
tegrates envelope area and internal volume. Window areas on south and 
west façades are particularly sensitive due to solar gain. The importance 
of window area (20 %) and window thermal conductivity (8 %) exceeds 
that of opaque material conductivity and thickness. This contradicts 
conventional strategies that prioritize insulation mass [4,50]. Overall, 
the results suggest that optimizing space layout and maximizing geo­
metric regularity at the design stage may deliver larger efficiency gains 
than material use alone.
The explicit modeling of component connections (edges) in 
BCGS reveals key heat transfer pathways. Centrality analysis shows 
that highly connected roofs and floors act as heat exchange hubs and 
require targeted design attention. In contrast, exterior walls in corner 
zones may experience reduced thermal load due to shading. These re­
sults highlight that energy-efficient design strategies must account for 
both physical attributes and topological positions.
5.2. Practical potential of BCGS
The proposed Enhanced GAT improves accuracy by 13 % compared 
to the end-to-end ensemble model XGBoost. The practical potential of 
this framework is not to replace physically transparent white-box 
simulation tools but to address the black-box nature of the design pro­
cess. The combination of BCGS and Enhanced GAT model offers repla­
ceability through graph structures, which not only accelerates energy 
prediction but also provides greater flexibility for energy-efficient 
design and retrofitting. In terms of efficiency, the Enhanced GAT 
model requires higher one-time training cost than XGBoost, but pre­
diction speed is similar and accuracy improves by 13 %. Compared with 
simulation engines such as EnergyPlus, this approach achieves predic­
tion efficiency several thousand times higher. These results demonstrate 
the potential of the BCGS to handle more complex and large-scale 
buildings, where efficiency becomes increasingly critical. 
• Wall and window attributes can be independently set for flexible 
component replacement.
• Decision-makers can identify critical heat transfer paths and priori­
tize retrofitting of high-risk components such as south-facing roofs.
• Spatial connectivity and thermo-optical efficiency can be optimized 
in the design phase to enhance overall energy performance.
Findings show that neglecting internal partitions leads to more than 
7 % prediction error. Ignoring external height variations results in about 
3.1 % error. These discrepancies increase as model complexity grows. By 
capturing detailed spatial configurations, BCGS closes this gap in con­
ventional modeling and provides a more realistic basis for simulating 
heat transfer and airflow. This approach with replaceable components is 
especially suitable for both retrofitting existing buildings and early-stage 
design. For example, BCGS can recommend enhanced spatial connec­
tivity. It can also guide the allocation of high-performance envelope 
materials to thermally sensitive zones such as south walls and roofs. In 
addition, it can match glazing performance to window size, helping to 
avoid energy inefficiency.
The BCGS framework serves as a data- and performance-driven aid 
for energy-efficient design, complementing rather than replacing the 
designer’s decision-making. Its efficacy relies on synergy with real- 
world design conditions, spatial reasoning, and parametric processes. 
As such, BCGS should be considered a targeted design support tool that 
connects performance feedback with design modifications at the 
component scale.
5.3. Limitations
At the data level, This study uses a single-story residential building 
with clear physical rules as a case study. The goal is to validate the BCGS 
graph structure, the learning performance of the GAT model, and the 
physical consistency of its interpretation. Reliability at this basic level is 
essential for future applications to more complex scenarios. The current 
work does not cover high-rise buildings, irregular forms, or group in­
teractions, which limits model generalization. High-quality 3D geome­
try data for buildings remain scarce. However, the flexible graph 
structure of BCGS supports multi-scale modeling from single dwellings 
to urban clusters. This helps overcome many limitations of data-driven 
BEM models in scene transfer and input formatting.
At the algorithm level, the current model does not include dynamic 
environmental variables such as weather or occupant behavior, nor 
system operating states. Yet these factors strongly influence energy use 
[14]. Incorporating multi-modal data fusion could improve the model’s 
adaptability across different contexts.
6. Conclusion
In response to the growing global challenges of building energy 
consumption and carbon neutrality, this study introduces and validates 
a novel framework. By representing components as graph nodes and 
their physical connections as edges, BCGS accurately captures three- 
dimensional spatial details and underlying physical relationships, 
addressing the limitations of classic BEM in spatial representation.
The key findings clearly demonstrate the advantages and new in­
sights enabled by BCGS. First, we quantify that ignoring spatial details 
such as interior partitions and height variation can lead to energy pre­
diction errors as high as 7.4 %, underscoring the necessity of fine- 
grained modeling. Second, using an Enhanced GAT specifically opti­
mized for BCGS data, we achieved a high prediction accuracy of R2 =
0.881, significantly outperforming conventional end-to-end data-driven 
approaches by 13 %.
Moreover, through GCNExplainer, the study reveals that geometric 
features and component connectivity have a greater and more direct 
influence on energy outcomes than traditionally emphasized thermal 
properties. The analysis also highlights differentiated effects across 
component types, with centrality metrics indicating that roofs and floors 
play distinct roles compared to walls. Beyond prediction, BCGS coupled 
with multi-objective optimization demonstrates strong potential in 
supporting energy-efficient design decisions and retrofit strategies. The 
framework forms a closed-loop workflow, from knowledge embedding 
and accurate prediction to interpretable analysis and design guidance.
In summary, the BCGS combined with graph neural networks pro­
vides a new paradigm for building energy prediction and optimization 
that is more accurate, interpretable, and practical. Its near-instant pre­
dictive capacity can greatly shorten performance analysis cycles, 
allowing designers to explore diverse schemes at low cost in the early 
stages and to balance energy use, cost, and comfort more effectively. On 
this basis, the BCGS framework also lays the foundation for AI-assisted 
generative design. In the future, it may enable the direct generation of 
building schemes that combine spatial rationality with optimal perfor­
mance under physical and topological constraints. This would move 
artificial intelligence from a role of performance evaluator to that of 
design collaborator, offering new momentum for low-carbon design and 
sustainable development in the building industry.
